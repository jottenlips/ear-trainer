## Why These Extensions?

### Mixolydian *or* Lydian Dominant — V7/V (D7 → G) and V7/IV (C7 → F)

These two secondary dominants live in a dual world. Both Mixolydian and Lydian Dominant work — they just give different colors. The trainer randomly picks one each time so you learn to hear both.

#### Mixolydian (9, 11, 13)

**The sound:** Bright, bluesy, uncomplicated. Think Jerry Garcia on *Fire on the Mountain*, or any classic rock dominant vamp. The extensions all come from the mixolydian mode built on the chord root.

**Why 9, 11, 13:** These are all diatonic — they sit inside the parent major scale without alteration. D7 in the key of C: the 9th is E (diatonic), the 11th is G (diatonic), the 13th is B (diatonic). No tension beyond the dominant 7th itself.

**Why natural 11 works here:** On most dominant chords, natural 11 (a half step above the 3rd) is an avoid note. But in a mixolydian context the chord often functions more as a modal sound than a hard V-I resolution. The 11th can float above the voicing without clashing because the 3rd is typically voiced lower. This is the most "inside" a secondary dominant can sound.

#### Lydian Dominant (9, #11, 13)

**The sound:** Floating, bright but sophisticated. The #11 gives it a lifted quality — not as dark as altered, not as plain as mixolydian.

**Why 9, #11, 13:** The lydian dominant scale (4th mode of melodic minor) raises the 11th to avoid the clash with the major 3rd while keeping the 9 and 13 natural.

**Why both work on II7 and I7:** D7 → G can sound like a bluesy mixolydian vamp (Dead, Allman Brothers) or a sophisticated lydian dominant approach (more Steely Dan). C7 → F is similar — the natural 11 is F (the target root) which works fine in a bluesy context but #11 (F#) creates more sophisticated tension. Context and genre determine which fits better, so the trainer exposes you to both.

**Real progressions (Mixolydian):**
- *Fire on the Mountain* (Grateful Dead) — B7 vamp with a mixolydian feel, extensions float freely
- Blues turnarounds — I7 → IV7 → V7, all mixolydian dominant sounds
- *Everyday* (Buddy Holly) — D → A7 → D, the A7 is V7/V territory with a pure mixolydian color

**Real progressions (Lydian Dominant):**
- *Steely Dan* — frequently uses II7 with #11 for that polished, lifted sound
- Fusion and modern jazz comping over II7 chords

### Lydian Dominant — SubV7/I (Db7 → C)

**The sound:** The definitive tritone substitute color. The #11 gives it a lifted quality that signals "I'm a sub, not a regular dominant."

**Why 9, #11, 13:** The lydian dominant scale (4th mode of melodic minor) raises the 11th to avoid the clash with the major 3rd while keeping the 9 and 13 natural. Db lydian dominant and G altered share the same melodic minor parent scale (Ab melodic minor).

**Why this is the tritone sub sound:** Db7 resolving to C has its 3rd (F) and 7th (Cb/B) forming the same tritone as G7 (B and F). The #11 of Db7 is G — the root of the V7 it's substituting for. The extensions of a lydian dominant tritone sub mirror the altered dominant they replace.

**Real progressions:**
- *Girl From Ipanema* — the Db7 in the bridge is a SubV7, pure lydian dominant color
- *Lady Bird* (Tadd Dameron) — uses tritone subs with lydian dominant extensions
- Any backdoor ii-V: Fm7 → Bb7 → Cmaj7, where Bb7 (bVII7) has a lydian dominant quality

---

### Altered — V7/ii (A7 → Dm), V7/vi (E7 → Am), V7/iii (B7 → Em)

**The sound:** Dark, tense, unstable. Maximum chromaticism against the parent key. Every extension is altered (raised or lowered) from its natural position. This is the sound of bebop and modern jazz tension.

**Why b9, #9, #11, b13:** These secondary dominants resolve to *minor* chords (ii, vi, iii). The altered extensions reflect the harmonic minor scale of the target key. Take V7/ii = A7 → Dm: thinking in D harmonic minor, A altered gives us Bb (b9), C (natural but functions as #9 enharmonically... actually #9 = B# = C), Eb (#11/b5), F (b13). All of these notes exist in D melodic or harmonic minor.

**Why NOT natural 11:** The natural 11 is 5 semitones above the root — exactly one half step above the major 3rd of the dominant chord. On A7, the 3rd is C#, and the natural 11 is D. That semitone clash doesn't sound "altered" or "tense," it sounds wrong. The #11 (D#/Eb) is the actual altered scale tone and creates the right kind of dissonance — chromatic tension that *wants* to resolve.

**The b9 is the telltale:** When you hear a dominant chord with a b9, your ear immediately codes it as "altered." The b9 creates a diminished sound against the root (root + b9 + 3 + 5 = a diminished 7th chord embedded in the voicing). This is why III7, VI7, and VII7 all sound distinctly darker than II7 or IV7.

**Real progressions:**
- *Autumn Leaves* — the E7 → Am (V7/vi) is the defining sound, extensions are altered
- *Stella by Starlight* — loaded with altered dominants resolving to minor chords
- *Blue Bossa* — the A7(b9) → Dm section, textbook altered secondary dominant
- Any minor ii-V-i: Bm7b5 → E7alt → Am, the E7 carries all the altered extensions

## Why the "Sound Category" Matters for Ear Training

The goal isn't to memorize which extensions go where — it's to hear the *color*:

1. **Mixolydian** sounds like home turned dominant. It's the blues. No surprise notes.
2. **Lydian Dominant** sounds like it's floating above the key. Bright but not inside.
3. **Altered** sounds like maximum tension. Dark, chromatic, pulling hard toward resolution.

When you toggle extensions on in the trainer, listen for these colors on the secondary dominant chord. The arpeggio plays the chord tones first (root, 3, 5, b7) then the extension color tones above. The extensions are what distinguish a V7/V from a V7/vi — the chord structure (dom7) is the same, but the *color on top* tells your ear where it wants to go.

## How It Connects to the ii-V-I Setup

The trainer plays: **Imaj7 → ii7 → V7 → Imaj7 → [SecDom] → [Target]**

The first four chords establish the key firmly in your ear. Then the secondary dominant arrives with its extensions painting the color, and your ear either:
- Hears mixolydian and thinks "this is just a dominant sound, probably going somewhere nearby"
- Hears lydian dominant and thinks "this is floating, maybe a tritone sub"
- Hears altered and thinks "this is dark and tense, resolving to a minor chord"

That instinct — color → destination — is what the extension playback mode trains.
